What Is Tax Residency?

Tax residency determines which country has the right to tax your worldwide income. It's separate from citizenship, separate from where your passport was issued, and separate from where you happen to be sitting when a client pays you. Tax residency is a legal status that typically depends on where you spend the most time, where your "center of life" is, or where you have the strongest personal and economic ties.

For most people with a fixed home, tax residency is obvious ‚Äî it's where they live. For digital nomads who split their year across multiple countries, it becomes genuinely complicated. And the consequences of getting it wrong are severe: double taxation, penalties, interest on unpaid taxes, and in extreme cases, criminal prosecution for tax evasion.

The irony is that most nomads aren't trying to evade taxes. They're just confused about who they owe money to. The international tax system wasn't designed for people who work from a laptop in a different country every month. The rules are catching up, but slowly.

Understanding tax residency starts with understanding how individual countries determine whether you're their tax resident. Most use a combination of physical presence (days spent), ties tests (family, property, bank accounts), and domicile concepts (where your "permanent home" is). These factors interact differently in every jurisdiction.

The 183-Day Rule

The most commonly cited threshold for tax residency is 183 days. Spend 183 days or more in a country within a tax year, and you're generally considered a tax resident there. This rule exists in some form in the vast majority of countries worldwide.

However, the 183-day rule is far from universal or simple:

  • Different counting methods: Some countries count calendar days (any part of a day = a full day). Others count overnight stays. Some use a "day of presence" test where you must be physically on the territory at midnight. The difference matters when you're close to the threshold.
  • Different tax years: Most countries use the calendar year (January 1 ‚Äì December 31). The UK uses April 6 ‚Äì April 5. Australia uses July 1 ‚Äì June 30. If you're splitting time between countries with different tax years, the same physical year of travel could trigger residency in one but not the other.
  • 183 days is a sufficient but not necessary condition: In many countries, you can become a tax resident with far fewer than 183 days. Having a "permanent home available" in France, for example, can make you a French tax resident regardless of days spent. The UK's Statutory Residence Test has pathways to residency starting at just 16 days if you have UK ties.
  • Some countries use different thresholds: Canada doesn't have a strict day count ‚Äî it uses a facts-and-circumstances test. The US uses a "substantial presence test" that weights days across three years using a formula. Some countries trigger residency at 90 days, not 183.
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical warning: The 183-day rule is a useful guideline, not a guarantee. Spending 182 days in a country does not automatically mean you're not a tax resident there. Always check the specific rules for each jurisdiction.

Beyond Day Counting

Many countries look at more than just days to determine tax residency. These additional factors can trigger residency even if you're under the day-count threshold:

Center of vital interests: This is a qualitative assessment of where your life is really based. Factors include: where your spouse/partner lives, where your children go to school, where your primary bank accounts are, where your social and cultural activities are, where you're registered to vote, where your car is registered, and where you have club memberships or religious affiliations.

Habitual abode: Even without hitting 183 days, if you regularly return to a country and it looks like your "habitual" place of living, some jurisdictions will claim you as a tax resident. This is more subjective and depends on patterns over multiple years.

Permanent home: Having a home available for your use ‚Äî even if you own or rent it and don't actually use it ‚Äî can trigger tax residency in some countries. France and Belgium are notably aggressive on this point. If you keep a rented apartment in Paris while nomading, France may consider you a tax resident.

Economic interests: Where do you earn your money? Where are your clients? Where is your company registered? If your economic activity is centered in one country, that country may claim tax residency even if you're not physically present for 183 days.

Intent and domicile: Common law countries (UK, US, Canada, Australia) often use a "domicile" concept separate from residence. Your domicile is your "permanent home" ‚Äî often the country your father was domiciled in when you were born, unless you actively establish a new domicile of choice. This can follow you for decades after leaving a country.

Dual Tax Residency

Here's where it gets particularly tricky for nomads: you can be a tax resident of two or more countries simultaneously. Country A might consider you resident because you spent 120 days there and have a bank account. Country B might consider you resident because your spouse lives there. Both want to tax your worldwide income.

Dual residency isn't just a theoretical edge case. It happens regularly to digital nomads, especially those who:

  • Split time roughly evenly between two countries
  • Maintain property or family ties in their home country while living abroad
  • Haven't formally established tax residence anywhere ("tax homeless" nomads)
  • Have a company registered in one country while living in another

Without resolution, dual residency means both countries tax your full worldwide income. You'd pay taxes twice on the same money. This is obviously unsustainable, which is why tax treaties exist.

But not all country pairs have tax treaties. And even when they do, relying on a treaty tiebreaker means you need to actively claim it ‚Äî it doesn't happen automatically. You need to understand the treaty, fill out the right forms, and potentially get a certificate of residence from one country to present to the other.

Tax Treaties and Tiebreakers

Tax treaties (also called Double Taxation Agreements or DTAs) are bilateral agreements between two countries that determine which one gets to tax you when both claim residence. Most treaties follow the OECD Model Tax Convention and use a standard tiebreaker sequence:

  1. Permanent home: You're resident where you have a permanent home available. If you have homes in both countries, move to the next test.
  2. Center of vital interests: Where are your personal and economic relations closest? Family, social, cultural, economic activities. If this is inconclusive, move on.
  3. Habitual abode: Where do you spend more time? If you spend more time in one country, you're resident there for treaty purposes.
  4. Nationality: If all else fails, your nationality breaks the tie. If you're a national of both, the countries must resolve it by mutual agreement.

The tiebreaker is applied sequentially ‚Äî you stop at the first test that gives a clear answer. In practice, "center of vital interests" is where most cases are decided, and it's also the most subjective and hardest to prove.

For nomads, the treaty tiebreaker process highlights why intentionally structuring your life around one country is so important. If your ties are scattered across multiple countries with no clear center, the determination becomes uncertain and potentially costly to litigate.

Citizenship-Based Taxation

Two countries in the world tax based on citizenship regardless of where you live: the United States and Eritrea. If you're a US citizen or green card holder, you owe US taxes on your worldwide income no matter where you live or how long you've been abroad.

For American digital nomads, this creates a unique situation. You can't escape US tax obligations by leaving. You can, however, use two important provisions:

Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE): In 2025, you can exclude approximately $130,000 of foreign earned income from US taxation if you meet either the Physical Presence Test (330 full days outside the US in a 12-month period) or the Bona Fide Residence Test (established residence in a foreign country for a full tax year).

Foreign Tax Credit (FTC): If you pay taxes to another country, you can credit those payments against your US tax liability. This prevents true double taxation in most cases, though the mechanics are complex.

American nomads also need to file FBAR (Foreign Bank Account Report) if foreign accounts exceed $10,000 in aggregate at any point during the year, and FATCA Form 8938 if foreign financial assets exceed certain thresholds. The penalties for not filing these information returns are severe ‚Äî up to $12,500 per account per year for FBAR, plus potential criminal penalties.

If you're American and nomading, you still need to file US taxes every year. A tax advisor experienced with expat returns is essentially mandatory.

Common Nomad Scenarios

Scenario 1: Left home country, no new base. You gave up your apartment in Canada and now travel perpetually. Canada may still consider you a tax resident based on remaining ties (bank accounts, driver's license, health insurance). Many nomads in this situation are technically "tax homeless" ‚Äî no country clearly claims them, but their home country often has a stronger argument than they think.

Scenario 2: Based in a low-tax country. You've established genuine residency in Portugal (NHR program), UAE (0% income tax), or Georgia (1% for small businesses). You have an apartment, a residency permit, and spend 183+ days there. This is the cleanest setup ‚Äî you have a clear tax home, and treaties protect you from double taxation in most cases.

Scenario 3: EU country-hopping. You spend 2-3 months in each of several EU countries. No single country hits 183 days. This is the riskiest scenario. Each country could potentially claim you as a resident based on non-day-count factors, or you could end up with no tax home at all (which can mean your home country retains its claim).

Scenario 4: US citizen abroad. You need to file US taxes regardless. Your goal is to either claim the FEIE (stay outside the US for 330+ days) or structure your affairs so foreign tax credits offset your US liability. You likely also need to establish tax residency somewhere for treaty benefits.

Country-Specific Rules Worth Knowing

Portugal: The Non-Habitual Resident (NHR) program offered favorable tax treatment for new residents, though it was substantially modified in 2024. Foreign-source income could be tax-exempt or taxed at a flat rate. Check current rules as they've evolved significantly.

UAE: No personal income tax, making it attractive for nomads. However, establishing genuine residency (Emirates ID, lease, utility bills) is important for treaty benefits with your home country. Simply having a UAE visa doesn't automatically make you a UAE tax resident for treaty purposes.

UK: The Statutory Residence Test (SRT) is one of the most complex in the world. It uses automatic overseas tests, automatic UK tests, and a "sufficient ties" test that cross-references days with various UK connections. A nomad with UK ties can be resident with as few as 16 days of presence.

Thailand: As of 2024, Thailand taxes worldwide income for residents who remit foreign income into the country (previously only Thai-source income was taxed for residents). This changes the calculus for nomads based in Thailand significantly. Spending 180+ days makes you a Thai tax resident.

Spain: The Beckham Law allows certain new residents to be taxed only on Spanish-source income for up to 6 years. There are also specific digital nomad visa provisions with tax implications. Spain uses 183 days as the threshold but also looks at economic interests and family ties.

Georgia: The "Small Business Status" allows individuals earning under ~155,000 GEL to pay just 1% tax. Combined with a relatively easy residency path and a 183-day threshold, Georgia has become a popular nomad tax base. However, Georgia's treaty network is smaller than some alternatives.

Compliance Strategies for Digital Nomads

1. Establish a clear tax home. The single most important thing you can do is pick one country and make it your unambiguous tax residence. Get the residency permit, sign a lease, open local bank accounts, register with tax authorities. When you have a clear tax home, everything else becomes simpler.

2. Clean up ties to your former country. If you've left your home country, actually leave. Close bank accounts you don't need, cancel health insurance registrations, surrender your driver's license if you can get one elsewhere, notify your tax authority that you've become a non-resident. Lingering ties create ambiguity.

3. Track your days obsessively. For every country you visit, know exactly how many days you've spent there. This is not just about visa compliance ‚Äî it's about tax residency thresholds. One day over 182 in the wrong country can trigger a full tax liability on your worldwide income.

4. Get professional advice. International tax is genuinely complex. A one-hour consultation with an expat tax specialist can save thousands in potential liabilities. The cost of professional advice is trivial compared to the cost of getting it wrong.

5. Keep records. Save boarding passes, hotel receipts, Airbnb confirmations, passport stamps ‚Äî anything that documents where you were on what dates. If a tax authority questions your residency, contemporaneous documentation is your best defense.

6. File proactively. Even if you believe you're not a tax resident of a country, consider filing a non-resident tax return or exit declaration. This creates a paper trail and starts statute of limitations clocks. Silence can be interpreted as evasion; proactive disclosure is almost always treated more favorably.

The most dangerous position is the one many nomads find themselves in: spending 4‚Äì5 months in their favorite country, feeling comfortable, not counting precisely, and then realizing they've triggered tax residency.
